Key Insights
On adoption failure:


Deployment ≠ adoption. Microsoft Copilot achieved only 1.8% conversion after 2 years and $billions in marketing. Amgen bought 20,000 licenses; employees kept using personal ChatGPT.
47% of employees using AI at work bypass enterprise tools entirely, using personal accounts with zero IT visibility.
The root cause of non-adoption is workflow friction, not lack of understanding — tools are portals layered on top of existing work rather than embedded inside it.


On production failure:


74–95% of AI projects never escape the pilot phase. The failure is organizational (no pre-defined success criteria, no business owner) not technical.
AI agents are being deployed into production systems faster than safety architecture is being built. The PocketOS incident (April 2026) — an agent deleting an entire customer database in 9 seconds — is the canonical example.
The "verification tax" is real and measurable: frequent AI users report higher burnout (45%) than non-users (35%), because reviewing AI output takes nearly as long as doing the task manually.


On governance:


Shadow AI is a structural data leakage crisis: 18% of enterprise employees paste corporate data into GenAI tools, 71.6% of that via non-corporate accounts. The top 25% of AI-using orgs experience 2,100+ data policy violations per month.
AI hallucinations carry legal liability. Courts have ruled companies are legally bound by what their AI tells customers, regardless of whether the AI "made it up." Deloitte paid a $290K reputational and financial price in 2025.
Only 32% of companies know how to measure AI ROI. 49% can't demonstrate value to financial stakeholders. ROI measurement almost universally starts after deployment, when it's too late to establish baselines.


On human behavior:


Resistance is existential, not informational. Training doesn't overcome threat responses. 31% of employees admit to actively sabotaging company AI initiatives.
Employee-chosen tools (GitHub Copilot, Notion AI, Superhuman) achieve adoption; employer-mandated tools don't. The difference is who the tool serves.


📌 Conclusions


The AI adoption problem is an organizational and human problem, not a technical one. Better models don't solve pilot purgatory, shadow AI, employee resistance, or ROI measurement gaps. Existing AI vendors are weakest exactly where the real problems live.
The market has a governance vacuum. Agent governance, pre-deployment ROI baselining, and employee-centric AI workflow tools are all undersupplied relative to demand. Every current observability/analytics tool is built developer-to-developer; the business buyer — who owns the risk and the budget — has no interface.
The biggest structural opportunity is the pre-deployment moment. No vendor currently occupies the space before AI is rolled out: defining outcomes, establishing baselines, scoping agent permissions. This is where downstream failures are determined, and it's unoccupied.
The "verification tax" reframes AI value. AI that reduces verification burden (honest uncertainty signals, grounded citations, sentence-level sourcing) is worth more in practice than AI that produces faster output of unknown reliability — especially in regulated or high-stakes domains.
Your product angle (organizational decision intelligence) is well-positioned. It sits in the layer where AI vendor weakness is highest: organizational context, rationale, accountability. This is the layer that survives commoditization of the model layer.
