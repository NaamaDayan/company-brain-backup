## Persona Quick Summary


The Series B CFO is standing at a concrete hiring decision point. The company has hit the size where vendor chaos is measurable, and someone on the leadership team has said "we need a procurement person." They have not posted the job yet — or they just did. They are evaluating three options: (1) make the hire, (2) give the problem to an existing VP Ops or COO, or (3) find a service that removes the need to hire at all.


This is the **headcount avoidance moment.** The budget already exists — it is the approved headcount budget for the role they are about to post. The economic buyer is the CFO, and the comparison is not against another software tool — it is against a $130–160K fully-loaded annual salary, a 3–6 month hiring process, and a 6-month ramp-up before any value is delivered.


**Why Series B specifically:**

- **Too early (Series A):** Below the procurement-chaos threshold (~50 employees). CEO handles vendor decisions. CFO does not feel acute pain yet.
- **Series B sweet spot:** 80–250 employees. Vendor stack has exploded. CFO has lost visibility. The pain is real and present.
- **Too late (Series C+):** Company likely already hired a Head of Procurement or VP Ops. A managed service disrupts an existing person. Sales cycle becomes political.

**The 3 target sub-archetypes within Series B:**

1. **The First Real CFO** — Just hired as first professional CFO (replacing a founder-as-CFO). Inherits vendor chaos. Wants to prove they can build infrastructure.
2. **The COO Who Inherited It** — Procurement fell to COO/VP Ops. Drowning. Accountable for OpEx discipline they cannot deliver without infrastructure.
3. **The CFO About to Post the Job** — Has drafted or approved a JD for a Procurement Manager. Closest to closing — has quantified the problem, just has not decided on the solution.

## Top 5 Burning Pains (Series B-Specific)

1. **The Hiring Decision Pain** — Being told "you need someone to own procurement" while knowing a full-time hire means 3–6 months to source, 6 months to ramp, $160K/year, and another headcount line the board will scrutinize.
2. **The Process Velocity Tax** — Approvals bottlenecked through CFO/COO. Engineers wait 2–3 weeks for a $200/month tool approval. Every hour spent on purchase approvals is stolen from financial modeling and board reporting.
3. **Shadow IT and Compliance Exposure** — Multiple teams independently trial and purchase tools. SOC2 audit finds unapproved SaaS. The CFO is accountable for OpEx they did not approve and cannot explain.
4. **The Fundraising Infrastructure Gap** — Series B investors expect a clear vendor contract register and controlled OpEx. Building this 6–12 months before Series C is the goal — not during the raise.
5. **The Full-Spend Blindspot** — Not just SaaS. The CFO does not know what the company pays for professional services, insurance, facilities, or hardware. Ramp/Brex show card spend only — not contract commitments or total vendor liability.

## Key Value Proposition Changes


**What we replace:** A full-time procurement hire — not a software tool.


**Speed:** Live in 2 weeks vs. 9–12 months before any value from a hire.


**Cost:** $5,500–8,000/month + % of savings vs. $130–160K salary + $26K recruiting fee.


**Scope vs. Spendflo:** All indirect procurement — professional services, insurance, HR vendors, hardware, facilities, SaaS. Spendflo does SaaS only.


**Risk:** Outcome-based pricing with savings guarantee. Zero downside to try.


**The pitch in one sentence:** "You were about to hire a Head of Procurement at $130K and 9 months of pain. We deliver the same output in 2 weeks at $6K/month, with a savings guarantee. If it does not work, you make the hire — and we have done your vendor audit for free."


**What NOT to say:** "We save you money on SaaS." This positions against Spendflo on their home turf. The frame is workforce replacement, not tool replacement.

## Discovery Interview Questions (v3.0 — Headcount Avoidance Frame)


### Part A — Hiring Decision Mapping

1. Has leadership discussed adding a procurement or operations person to the team? Where does that conversation stand?
2. If you were to post a Head of Procurement role today — what would you need them to do in their first 90 days?
3. What is holding you back from making that hire right now?
4. If someone could deliver the same output as that hire, starting next week, at 40% of the cost — what would make you skeptical?

### Part B — Current Procurement Reality

1. Walk me through what happens when a team lead wants to buy a new tool or service.
2. How many vendors does the company pay today across all spend categories — not just SaaS?
3. When did you last discover a vendor contract or commitment you did not know existed?
4. What percentage of your week goes to procurement-related work?

### Part C — Pain Validation

1. What is the most expensive procurement mistake your company has made in the last 12 months?
2. Is there spend happening outside your visibility — teams buying things without finance knowing?
3. How do you feel about your SOC2/compliance posture around vendor onboarding and shadow IT?

### Part D — Budget and Decision Process

1. What would you expect to pay for a service that ran your procurement function?
2. Who would need to be involved in approving that decision?
3. What would success look like 6 months after getting procurement under control?
