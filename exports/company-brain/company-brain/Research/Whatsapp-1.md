Key Insights
There is a growing thesis that traditional SaaS is being replaced by “managed AI services”, where vendors don’t sell tools but operate full workflows end-to-end (black-box delivery of outcomes).
A cyber startup example reflects this shift: instead of selling security platforms, they embed engineers/AI agents to fully operate security processes for clients, reducing need for internal DIY.
This “services-as-software” model aligns with ideas from Sequoia on outsourcing:
If a task is already outsourced, it’s easier to replace (clear budget, defined outcomes, vendor swap vs org redesign).
In procurement specifically, companies like Keelvar and Omnea illustrate different approaches to procurement automation:
Keelvar: focused on sourcing automation niche (mature, enterprise-heavy, long-standing player).
Omnea: focuses on procurement orchestration and ROI-driven adoption.
A newer AI-native player example, [Lio.ai](http://lio.ai/), represents the “fully managed procurement team” approach:
Replacing outsourced procurement (BPO/shared services) rather than internal employees.
High autonomy with human-in-the-loop mainly for approvals.
Strong ROI narrative (cost savings, headcount reduction, fast onboarding).
The market is increasingly crowded in adjacent automation spaces (legal AI, AP/AR, procurement SaaS), making differentiation harder.
Mid-market procurement differs from enterprise:
Less outsourcing, more internal small teams → harder ROI narrative based on headcount reduction.
Strong signal: practitioners report high inbound noise in procurement AI space, indicating saturation.
Potential expansion areas beyond procurement:
Insurance back-office, staffing operations, industrial procurement, wholesale distribution.
Alternative GTM idea: selling to BPO providers themselves, replacing or augmenting their delivery layer.
Conclusions
The most promising wedge is likely replacing existing outsourced workflows (BPO) rather than internal headcount, since it creates clearer ROI and easier substitution.
“Managed AI operations” is emerging as a distinct category between SaaS and BPO: vendors become operators, not tool providers.
Procurement (especially enterprise SaaS angle) is likely highly competitive and crowded, but specific sub-segments (industrial, mid-market, outsourcing-heavy verticals) may still offer whitespace.
The key strategic constraint is not only the product idea, but finding the right vertical + buyer archetype where outsourcing already exists and is budgeted.
Hypotheses
H1: Vertical AI companies that replace BPO providers will scale faster than those replacing internal teams, due to clearer ROI and easier budget substitution.
H2: Mid-market companies with outsourced operations (not enterprise SaaS-heavy orgs) are the best early GTM segment for AI-native “managed services.”
H3: Procurement mid-market (without outsourcing) may be harder to monetize via headcount reduction, requiring alternative ROI framing (efficiency, spend optimization).
H4: “AI operations companies” that act as black-box workflow owners will eventually converge into a new category distinct from SaaS and traditional services.
H5: Industries with structured, repeatable workflows (insurance back-office, staffing ops, wholesale distribution) will show higher adoption than knowledge-heavy domains.
