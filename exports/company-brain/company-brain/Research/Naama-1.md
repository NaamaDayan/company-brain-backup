Key Insights


The core business thesis is "Service-as-Software" — selling outcomes, not tools, by running AI-native managed units on behalf of clients
The entry strategy is a "Trojan Horse": land via one high-volume, painful workflow (e.g. invoice processing), prove ROI, then expand via a Strangler Fig model until owning an entire department
The white space is real and confirmed by research: no well-funded player occupies the bespoke + managed-outcome quadrant simultaneously
Invisible Technologies is the closest analog ($134M revenue, human-in-loop + AI platform) but remains horizontal — serving everyone from NBA teams to the US Navy with no vertical wedge playbook
Palantir has the FDE (Forward Deployed Engineer) on-the-ground insight but sells an enterprise platform license, not a managed outcome
Harvey AI proves vertical moats work ($190M ARR, legal-only), but still sells software seats
Traditional consulting (Accenture, McKinsey) is structurally misaligned: $300–500/hr day rates and 6–18 month timelines are the exact gap to exploit
The macro is timing-perfect: enterprises are shifting from FTE-based to outcome-based contracts


Conclusions


The two decisive axes in this market are: how you deliver (tool vs. managed outcome) and how specialized you are (horizontal vs. bespoke) — and the top-right quadrant (bespoke + outcome-based) is unoccupied
Adoption resistance (the "Adoption Wall") is best bypassed by framing the AI unit as a "VIP Lane" or support layer, not a replacement
Renovating a legacy department is harder than building a parallel AI-native one alongside it — the Strangler Fig approach is structurally superior to transformation consulting
VC signal is strong: the service-as-software framing aligns with a16z's publicly stated thesis on the next wave of B2B
